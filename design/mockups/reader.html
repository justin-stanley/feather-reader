Notes on running a small, calm web service for a decade

8 min read · example.com

Ten years ago I deployed a small web service on a five-dollar VPS and promised myself I would keep it alive for a decade. Last month it quietly crossed that line. Nobody noticed, which is the highest compliment infrastructure can receive.

What follows is not a heroic story. It is a list of the things that actually mattered, most of which I would not have guessed at the start — and almost none of which involve the application code.

The code was never the problem

In ten years I made forty-one deploys. Six of them fixed bugs; the rest were dependency updates and two migrations. The application itself — a few thousand lines behind a reverse proxy — has been the most reliable component of the whole system by an enormous margin.

The half-life of enthusiasm is shorter than the half-life of software. Design for the person who has stopped caring, because in year seven, that person is you.

What broke instead, in descending order of drama: TLS certificates (twice, before ACME), a full disk (log rotation I swore I had configured), and a hosting provider acquisition that required an evening of migration.

Boring choices compound

Every technology choice was made with one question: will this still be maintained when I am not paying attention? That ruled out most of what was exciting in 2016, and the survivors look almost embarrassingly plain:

  • One static binary, supervised by the init system — nothing to orchestrate.
  • SQLite, backed up nightly with a single sqlite3 .backup call.
  • Plain-text logs, rotated, greppable at 3 a.m. with no dashboard between us.

The whole restore procedure fits in a shell script that has aged better than most of my opinions:

#!/bin/sh
# restore.sh — the entire disaster-recovery plan
scp backup:/srv/backups/app-$(date +%F).db ./app.db
./app --db ./app.db --listen :8080

There is a lesson in the fact that the script is nine lines and the runbook for the Kubernetes cluster at my day job is nine pages.

Calm is a budget you spend

The service costs me about forty minutes a year now. That number is the product of every boring decision above, and it is the only metric I still track. Uptime is a lagging indicator; attention spent is the leading one.

If you are starting something small this weekend: pick the dull database, write the nine-line restore script, and set a calendar reminder for 2036. The web needs more services that simply, quietly, keep working.
